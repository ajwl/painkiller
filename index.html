Pill-popping in the UK, how much are we spending to feel better?

Spend on over-the-counter drugs in the UK, 2009-2014 (£m)

Click a button to see how much we spend on different types of medicaments

All drugs

  • Was it a warm winter in 2011? Spending on cough and cold medication dropped noticeably
  • Spending on painkillers has climbed steadily since 2011, increasing by £50m (9%) in 3 years
  • 2011 was a recent low for digestive remedy spending too, it rose £13m (4%) in the 3 years after that

Painkillers

  • Brits take a lot of systemic painkillers like Ibuprofen and paracetemol and we spent over half a billion pounds on them in 2014 (£504m)
  • Again, 2014 had the highest systemic painkiller spend in recent years, was it a particularly painful year for Brits?
  • We've been rubbing a whole lot more more painkillers onto ourselves recently too: spending on topical painkillers is up 33% (£24m) between 2009 and 2014
  • We've spent increasingly more on getting to sleep since 2010, a rise of almost 20% (£9.2m) in 4 years

Coughs, colds and allergy medicine

  • Suffer from allergies? Look like we're spending more to save us from sneezing - spending on antihistamines has increased by a third in five years: a rise of 36% or £24m
  • Spending on cough remedies and throat medication has stayed pretty constant over the last five years - even falling slightly in the case of adult cough meds

Digestive remedies

  • IBS medicine has seen a big boom in the last five years - spending on treating Irritable Bowel Syndrome is up a huge 76% between 2009 and 2014
  • 2010 was the peak year for laxatives - and spending has gone down slightly since then
  • In contrast diarrhoea spending has gone up and is now almost level with laxatives, though laxatives have been ahead for the last five years. Diarrhoea spending went up 10% (£5m) between 2010 and 2014.

Note on the data

Please note that spending on drugs could indicate increased prices as well as increased consumption.

As we understand it, most over-the-counter medicines as self-administered by patients ARE subject to full VAT. VAT went up to 20% from 15% in January 2011
